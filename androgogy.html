Overview & Purpose

This portal treats andragogy as a design skill, not a term to memorize. Work through the labs, redesign one real element of your teaching, and walk away with a reusable checklist.

Big Outcomes for Faculty

Approx. 60–90 minutes (modular)
  • Look at any adult-learning situation and quickly spot where it aligns or clashes with andragogy.
  • Redesign real courses, assignments, or workshops to better fit adult learners under real constraints.
  • Explain why a design choice works (or fails) using andragogical principles, not intuition alone.

How to use this: Move section by section, or cherry-pick a lab to use in a department meeting or teaching workshop.

Core Andragogy Lens

Instead of memorizing a list, treat these principles as if–then rules you can test in your own context.

Andragogy Principles as Design Rules

  • Need to know: If adults see the why behind learning, they’re far more likely to invest effort.
  • Self-concept (autonomy): If adults experience real choice or control, resistance drops and engagement rises.
  • Prior experience: If you surface and use their experiences, learning feels relevant and deeper.
  • Readiness to learn: If learning is tied to current roles and goals, adults lean in.
  • Orientation to learning (problem-centered): If you anchor learning in concrete problems, adults stay with it.
  • Intrinsic motivation: If learning connects to identity, values, and growth, motivation sustains beyond grades.
Design question: For any activity you run, can you point to which of these principles you’re intentionally leveraging—and which you are ignoring?

Lab 1 – Diagnose Your Mental Model

Goal: Surface your current assumptions about adult learners and see where they clash with andragogy.

Step 1: Quick Gut-Check

Individual reflection

Answer these prompts in a notebook, document, or LMS journal. Don’t overthink.

  1. When adults seem “unmotivated,” my default explanation is usually:
    • a) They’re busy or overwhelmed
    • b) The course feels irrelevant or boring
    • c) They’re just not that interested in learning
    • d) Something else: write your own
  2. When I design learning for adults, I tend to focus most on:
    • a) Covering required content
    • b) Giving practice and feedback
    • c) Providing choice and relevance
    • d) Meeting assessment or compliance requirements
  3. In practice, how much control do my adult learners really have?
    • a) Almost none
    • b) Some choice, but tightly framed
    • c) Meaningful control over path or tasks
    • d) We co-create quite a bit
  4. One place where adults in my context consistently struggle or resist is:
    Example: discussion boards, group projects, compliance training, weekly reflections, etc.

Step 2: Identify the Tensions

Now compare your answers to the core principles above and ask:

  • Where do my answers clash with the andragogy principles?
  • Where am I assuming I’m “doing andragogy” simply because my learners are adults?

Capture three honest bullets:

  • Here’s where my current practice probably violates andragogy:
    • 1. _____________________________
    • 2. _____________________________
    • 3. _____________________________
Use these bullets as a “before” snapshot. You’ll revisit them in Lab 4 after redesigning something real.

Lab 2 – Principle Puzzles

Goal: Practice spotting where andragogy is being honored or violated in realistic scenarios.

Scenario 1 – Mandatory Webinar Hell

Try first, then reveal analysis

You run a 90-minute Zoom webinar on “Data Security Best Practices” for staff. Slides are dense, you lecture most of the time, attendance is mandatory, cameras are off, and participants later report: “Repetitive,” “Not relevant,” “I could’ve watched this at 2x speed.”

Your task

  1. Which andragogy principles are clearly being violated?
  2. What is one concrete change you could make that does not require more time or budget?

Model Analysis

  • Need to know: The “why this matters for my job” is vague or missing.
  • Problem-centered orientation: The session is topic-centered (“Data Security 101”), not organized around real incidents or decisions.
  • Self-concept (autonomy): Participation is mandatory, interaction is minimal, and there’s no meaningful choice.

Low-cost design pivots:

  • Open with 2–3 realistic breach scenarios from your organization and ask, “What would you do?”
  • Let participants choose one scenario to explore in small groups, then connect your key points directly to their responses.

Reflect: Do you agree those are the main principles at stake, or would you argue a different primary issue?

Scenario 2 – Overloaded Master’s Students

Working professionals in an online course

You teach an online master’s course. Students are working full time. Each week includes heavy readings, two discussion posts plus replies, and a paper every two weeks. Many students are late and say “life got in the way.”

Your task

  1. Which principles might actually be respected here?
  2. Which are being underused or ignored?
  3. What is one change that uses students’ prior experience instead of simply adding more content?

Model Analysis

Potentially respected:

  • Readiness to learn: Students chose this degree, so content likely aligns with their professional goals.
  • Intrinsic motivation: Time and money invested suggest at least baseline motivation.

Underused:

  • Prior experience: Assignments often treat them like novices instead of practitioners.
  • Self-concept: Little flexibility in how they demonstrate learning.
  • Problem-centered orientation: Heavy on “read and talk about content,” lighter on solving real problems from their contexts.

Example pivot:

  • Replace one weekly discussion with: “Bring a real problem from your workplace that connects to this week’s reading. Briefly describe it, apply one concept, and propose one concrete experiment you could try next week.”

Challenge yourself: Is the issue simply “too much work,” or “the wrong kind of work” for adult learners?

Lab 3 – Case Studies & Design Fixes

Goal: Move from diagnosis to concrete redesigns in faculty-facing and student-facing contexts.

Case Study A – Skeptical Faculty Workshop

Workshop redesign

You’re running a 90-minute workshop on “Using AI to Support Student Learning.” Many faculty are skeptical or anxious and feel pushed by administration. Your original plan is: 30 minutes presentation, 30 minutes demo, 30 minutes Q&A.

Design Challenge

Rebuild the 90 minutes so it reflects at least four andragogy principles.

  1. Opening (10–15 min): How will you surface their prior experiences, concerns, and goals instead of starting with a lecture?
  2. Core activities (50–60 min): Where do they get choice in tools, topics, or tasks? How will you make it problem-centered (their courses, not your slides)?
  3. Closing (10–15 min): How will you connect this to their intrinsic motivations as teachers (values, identity, disciplines)?

Example Redesign Sketch

  • Opening: Short poll + paired share: “Where are you right now with AI (avoidant, curious, experimenting, etc.)? What’s one real tension you feel?” Facilitator maps tensions to andragogy principles (autonomy, relevance, etc.).
  • Core: Faculty choose one real course of theirs. In small groups, they map one concrete pain point (grading load, feedback quality, access, etc.) and experiment with AI workflows that address their problems, not generic demos.
  • Closing: Quick reflection: “What’s one small, value-consistent experiment you’re willing to run next term?” Emphasize alignment with their teaching identity, not institutional pressure.

Case Study B – Redesign a Dull Assignment

Course assignment redesign

Pick a real assignment you use with adult learners (or adapt this example):

“Write a 5-page paper summarizing three theories of motivation.”

Run it through the andragogy challenge below and rewrite it.

Andragogy Challenge

  1. Need to know: How obvious is the answer to “Why should I care about doing this now?” Rewrite the prompt to make the “why” explicit.
  2. Self-concept (autonomy): Where can you add a meaningful choice (topic, artifact format, workplace context)?
  3. Prior experience: Add a step requiring learners to connect theories to their own professional history or decisions.
  4. Problem-centered orientation: Turn the abstract summary into a concrete problem (e.g., “A colleague is burning out; design a plan using two theories.”).
  5. Intrinsic motivation: Include a brief reflection: “Which theory best explains you at your best/worst? How will that shape how you lead or teach?”

Example Reframed Assignment

New prompt (sketch):

“Using at least two motivation theories from this module, analyze a real motivation challenge in your own course or workplace. Describe the situation, apply the theories to explain what’s happening, and design a concrete intervention you could implement in the next four weeks. Present your work as either a written brief, a slide deck, or a short recorded presentation.”

Lab 4 – Real-World Redesign Sprint

Goal: Choose one concrete element of your teaching, audit it using andragogy, and commit to a high-leverage change.

Step 1: Choose a Testbed

Pick one context where you actually have power to change something:

  • A course you teach this term
  • A recurring training or workshop
  • Orientation or professional development sessions
  • One specific activity (e.g., online discussion, reflection, quiz, group project)

Step 2: Andragogical Audit

Fill in the table below (copy into a doc or LMS if helpful) to map current practice against andragogy.

Element Current State (What You Actually Do) Principles Supported / Violated Possible Change
Opening of a session or course
Major assignment / task
Feedback & assessment
Use of learners’ prior experience
Opportunities for choice / autonomy

Prompt:

  • Where are you unintentionally treating adults like compliant children?
  • Where are you already leaning into andragogy but could make it more explicit?

Step 3: Commit to One High-Leverage Change

Instead of trying to fix everything, identify one change with high impact and realistic effort.

  • Convert a “syllabus + lecture” first session into a co-constructed expectations and goals conversation.
  • Swap one content-heavy assignment for a workplace-based project.
  • Offer choice of artifact format (paper, video, proposal) while keeping the same learning outcomes.

Write a design statement:

“In [context], I will change [X] to [Y] because it better supports [named andragogy principles]. I expect this will change learner behavior in [specific way].”
