Lesson 1 – From Command Lines to Generative AI

Reading: A Short History of How We Talk to Machines

2–4 minute read

On campus today, it’s easy to forget that not long ago, using a computer meant memorizing strict commands and typing them into a blinking prompt. Over time, the way we interact with machines has shifted through three big interface eras: command-line interfaces (CLI), graphical user interfaces (GUI), and today’s natural language interfaces (NLI) powered by generative AI.

Command-Line Interfaces (CLI)

In the CLI era, users typed exact commands like mkdir assignments or ls to see files. This approach is powerful and efficient, but unforgiving. You had to remember syntax, and a single typo could cause an error or unexpected result. On campus, this often meant only technically trained users or computing staff could fully benefit.

Graphical User Interfaces (GUI)

GUIs brought windows, icons, menus, and pointing devices. Instead of typing print myfile.txt, you could click a printer icon. This made computing more accessible across campus: students, faculty, and staff could use software without knowing every command. GUIs shifted expertise from “remembering commands” to “navigating interfaces.”

Natural Language Interfaces (NLI) and GenAI

Today, with tools like ChatGPT and other campus-approved platforms, we’re entering an NLI era. Instead of clicking or typing commands, you can say or type: “Summarize this article for a first-year student,” or “Help me brainstorm outreach ideas for a campus event.” The interface is now conversation. Under the hood, these tools are not magical minds—they are statistical models trained to continue text in plausible ways.

From Rule-Based Systems to Generative AI

Before GenAI, many “smart” systems on campus were rule-based: people encoded “if this, then that” logic, such as sending an alert when a student’s grade fell below a threshold. Early AI and machine learning models learned patterns from data, but were often narrow and specialized.

Generative AI models are trained on vast amounts of text (and sometimes code, images, or audio) and can produce new text, images, or other media that follow the patterns they’ve observed. The leap is not that they “understand,” but that they are very good at pattern-based continuation and recombination.

Key idea: Each interface shift (CLI → GUI → NLI) makes computing feel more natural, but also hides more of what’s happening underneath. As we use GenAI on campus, we need to add new literacy about how these tools behave.

Lesson 2 – Pattern Machines, Not Minds

Reading: What GenAI Is (and Is Not)

2–4 minute read

Generative AI tools can sound confident, conversational, and even funny. It’s tempting to talk about them as if they “know” things or “understand” us. But under the hood, large language models are best understood as pattern machines that work on tokens and probabilities—not as minds.

Tokens: The Building Blocks

When you type a prompt, the model doesn’t see full sentences the way you do. It breaks your text into tokens—small units such as words or parts of words. It then predicts, step by step, which token is most likely to come next given all the tokens it has seen so far.

Patterns, Not Facts

Because the model is trained on large amounts of text, it learns patterns about which words tend to appear together. When you ask a question, it is not looking up a fact in a database. It is generating a sequence of tokens that fit the patterns it has seen, shaped by your prompt.

Prompts as Steering Wheels

Your prompt is the steering wheel. The more clearly you describe your role, your audience, your constraints, and your goal, the more you shape which patterns the model uses. Vague prompts invite vague patterns. Specific prompts invite more focused patterns—though they still require checking.

No Understanding, No Intentions

The model does not have beliefs, experiences, or intentions. It does not know when it is wrong. Its outputs can be useful and insightful, but that is because patterns in the training data reflect human knowledge—not because the model itself “understands” in a human sense.

Key idea: Treat GenAI output as a highly sophisticated autocomplete. It can be extremely helpful, but it cannot replace human understanding, judgment, or verification.

Lesson 3 – Limits, Risks & Guardrails

Reading: Why Care About Limits?

2–4 minute read

Generative AI tools can be helpful, but they also bring real limitations and risks. On a university campus, where accuracy, ethics, and equity matter, it’s important to understand these patterns so we can build guardrails.

Bias and Representation

Models learn from large datasets that reflect the world as it is, including its inequalities and stereotypes. As a result, they can reproduce or amplify biased language and assumptions about people based on race, gender, disability, age, or other characteristics.

Hallucinations

A “hallucination” is when the model generates a fluent, detailed answer that is simply wrong or made up. This can include fake citations, incorrect dates, or invented quotes. The model is not lying; it is following patterns that look like answers.

Context Windows

Models can only “see” a limited number of tokens at once—this is called the context window. Long documents, long conversations, or many pasted texts may be summarized or truncated in ways you cannot see directly. Important details can be lost or misrepresented.

Lack of Understanding and Accountability

The model does not understand consequences or ethics. It does not know campus policies, legal requirements, or your personal values unless you bring them into the conversation and still check the output against trusted sources.

Key idea: Using GenAI responsibly means assuming it will sometimes be biased or wrong, and designing your workflow to catch and correct those issues.

Lesson 4 – Creativity, Brainstorming & Temperature

Reading: Using GenAI as a Brainstorm Partner

2–4 minute read

Generative AI can be a powerful ally for brainstorming: generating options, reframing problems, and surfacing ideas you might not think of on your own. The goal is not to let the tool decide for you, but to give you more raw material to evaluate.

Creative Uses on Campus

  • Drafting multiple versions of event titles or outreach messages.
  • Brainstorming project ideas, research questions, or group activities.
  • Exploring alternative explanations of a concept for different audiences.

Temperature and Creativity

Many GenAI tools expose a setting called temperature (or a similar “creativity” slider). Lower temperatures push the model toward safer, more predictable completions. Higher temperatures encourage more varied and surprising outputs.

When you want precision (e.g., summarizing policy language), you typically want lower temperature and very clear prompts. When you want divergent brainstorming (e.g., wild ideas for a themed event), you can move the temperature higher or ask explicitly for unusual, experimental options.

Key idea: You remain the editor and decision-maker. AI can help you think wider and faster, but you choose what to keep, adapt, or discard.

Prompt-based activity

Copy-and-paste prompts (run them as two separate prompts; adjust if your tool has an explicit temperature slider):

  • Which would you adapt—and how?
  • Which go too far for your context, and why?
