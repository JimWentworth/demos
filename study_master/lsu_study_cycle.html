The Power of Retrieval Practice

Retrieval practice is the act of recalling information from memory rather than simply re-reading it. Research shows this is one of the most effective learning techniques and forms the foundation of the Study Cycle.

Active recall builds stronger neural pathways than passive review. Each time you retrieve information, you're strengthening your ability to recall it in the future.

Benefits of Retrieval Practice:

  • Strengthens memory connections - Makes information easier to recall in the future
  • Identifies knowledge gaps - Shows you exactly what you need to review
  • Prevents illusions of competence - Avoids false confidence from simply recognizing material
  • Improves long-term retention - Helps you remember information for exams and beyond
  • Enhances transfer of knowledge - Helps you apply concepts to new contexts

The LSU Study Cycle below integrates retrieval practice principles at each step, creating a comprehensive approach to learning that maximizes retention and understanding.

The LSU Study Cycle

Follow these five steps to maximize your learning through structured retrieval practice. Each step builds upon the previous one to create stronger memory connections.

1

Preview

Before class
▼

What to do:

Before class, take 10-15 minutes to skim the material that will be covered. Note headings, bold terms, charts, and summaries.

Why it works:

This creates mental structures that make it easier for you to organize and understand new information during the lecture. It's like creating folders in your brain before filing the information.

Retrieval connection:

Even basic familiarity with concepts creates initial memory traces that make future retrieval easier.

Quick tips:

  • Read chapter summaries and learning objectives
  • Scan headings, subheadings, and bold terms
  • Review any charts, diagrams, or illustrations
  • Write down questions you hope will be answered in class
2

Attend

Go to class
▼

What to do:

Attend class regularly and be an active participant. Take meaningful notes that capture key concepts rather than trying to write everything down verbatim.

Why it works:

Active engagement improves attention and begins the process of memory formation. The more you engage with the material, the deeper your understanding will be.

Retrieval connection:

Asking and answering questions during class is a form of retrieval practice that strengthens learning in real-time.

Quick tips:

  • Sit near the front of the class to minimize distractions
  • Ask questions when you don't understand something
  • Use abbreviations and symbols in your notes to be more efficient
  • Leave space in your notes for additions during review
  • Highlight connections between new material and what you already know
3

Review

After class
▼

What to do:

As soon as possible after class (ideally within 24 hours), spend 10-15 minutes reviewing your notes. Fill in gaps, clarify confusing points, and identify key concepts.

Why it works:

This step prevents forgetting and strengthens neural connections while the material is still fresh in your mind. Research shows that we forget up to 50% of new information within 24 hours if we don't review it.

Retrieval connection:

Try to rewrite key points from memory first before reviewing your notes to engage in active retrieval.

Quick tips:

  • Close your notes and write a brief summary of the main points
  • Create summary notes using your own words
  • Mark areas that are still confusing for further study
  • Connect new information with concepts from previous classes
  • Begin creating flashcards for key terms or concepts
4

Study

Practice recall
▼

What to do:

Set aside dedicated time for intense, focused study sessions using active learning techniques, especially retrieval practice (recalling information from memory) rather than passive re-reading.

Why it works:

Retrieval practice strengthens memory and makes future recall easier. It's also much more effective than re-reading, which can create an illusion of competence without true learning.

Retrieval connection:

This is the most direct application of retrieval practice in the cycle. The act of recalling information forces your brain to strengthen those neural pathways.

Quick tips:

  • Use flashcards to test yourself on key concepts
  • Close your notes and write down everything you remember about a topic
  • Explain concepts out loud as if teaching someone else
  • Create practice tests or use old exams to test your knowledge
  • Use the Pomodoro technique (25-minute focused sessions)
  • Vary your study environment to improve retention
5

Check

Assess learning
▼

What to do:

Regularly assess whether you truly understand the material. Can you explain concepts clearly without looking at your notes? Can you apply concepts to new situations?

Why it works:

This step helps identify knowledge gaps and prevents overconfidence. It ensures that you're actually learning, not just recognizing material.

Retrieval connection:

Testing your understanding in new contexts is an advanced form of retrieval practice that builds deeper understanding and versatile knowledge.

Quick tips:

  • Try teaching the material to someone else (or an imaginary student)
  • Create concept maps showing relationships between ideas
  • Solve practice problems without referring to examples
  • Write out explanations of complex concepts in simple language
  • Identify areas where you're struggling and return to the Study step

Repeat the cycle for continuous improvement through spaced retrieval practice
