üîç Activity 1: AI as a Pattern Machine

15 minutes

Quick Concept: What GenAI Actually Does

Generative AI isn't "thinking" or "understanding" in the way humans do. It's a sophisticated pattern-matching system that:

  • Breaks text into tiny pieces called tokens
  • Predicts what token should come next based on patterns in its training data
  • Builds responses token by token, using probability calculations
Think of it like this: If I say "peanut butter and ___", you'd likely say "jelly." Not because you understand sandwiches, but because you've seen that pattern many times. AI works similarly, but with billions of patterns!

Try This: The Pattern Test

Let's test how AI handles patterns it has learned versus ones it hasn't. Open your AI tool and try these three prompts in order:

  1. Test 1 - Common Pattern:
    Complete this phrase: "To be or not to be, that is the ___"
  2. Test 2 - Less Common:
    What year did the University of Illinois hire its current Dean of Students?
  3. Test 3 - Novel Pattern:
    My colleague Jim at UIUC loves developing Canvas LMS integrations. What was his most recent project about?

What Did You Notice?

üí≠ Discussion Questions

  • Which prompts gave confident answers? Which ones were more uncertain?
  • Did the AI tell you when it didn't know something, or did it make something up?
  • What does this tell us about when to trust AI responses?
Key Takeaway: AI is excellent at patterns it has seen frequently (like famous quotes) but struggles with specific, unique, or recent information. It may "hallucinate" answers rather than admit uncertainty.

üí° Activity 2: The Power of Prompting

20 minutes

Quick Concept: Your Prompt = AI's Instructions

The way you phrase your prompt dramatically affects the response. Think of prompting like giving directions:

  • Vague: "Help me with dinner" ‚Üí Could mean anything!
  • Clear: "Suggest 3 vegetarian dinner recipes under 30 minutes" ‚Üí Much more useful!

Prompting Techniques That Work

üéØ Be Specific: Include context, constraints, and desired format
üé≠ Assign a Role: "You are an experienced teacher..." or "Act as a technical writer..."
üìã Give Examples: Show the AI what you want with 1-2 examples
üî¢ Request Structure: Ask for numbered lists, tables, or specific formats

Try This: Prompt Improvement Challenge

We'll take a vague prompt and improve it step by step. Try each version and compare results!

Round 1: The Vague Prompt

Write something about teaching with AI.

Try this in your AI tool. What kind of response do you get?

Round 2: Adding Context & Specificity

I'm a college instructor teaching first-year biology. Write 3 specific ways I could use AI tools to support my students' learning without replacing authentic assessment.

How did the response change?

Round 3: Adding Role & Format

You are an experienced instructional designer specializing in science education. I'm a college instructor teaching first-year biology to 150 students. Write 3 specific ways I could use AI tools to support my students' learning without replacing authentic assessment. For each suggestion, include: 1) The tool or approach, 2) A concrete example, 3) One potential drawback to consider. Format as a numbered list.

Notice the difference in quality and usefulness!

üõ°Ô∏è Activity 3: Recognizing Limits & Setting Guardrails

20 minutes

Quick Concept: Where AI Falls Short

AI is a powerful tool, but it has real limitations and risks we need to understand:

‚ö†Ô∏è Hallucinations AI confidently invents facts, citations, or data that sound plausible but are completely wrong.
‚öñÔ∏è Bias Training data reflects historical biases, leading to unfair or stereotyped outputs.
üîí Privacy Concerns Information you share may be used to train future models or stored in ways you don't expect.
üìÖ Outdated Information Most AI models have knowledge cutoff dates and don't know current events.

Try This: Spotting Hallucinations

Let's deliberately trigger hallucinations to see what they look like:

  1. Ask for specific citations:
    List 3 peer-reviewed research articles from 2023 about the effectiveness of AI tutors in undergraduate mathematics, including full citations.

    Check: Do these papers actually exist? Try searching for the titles or DOIs.

  2. Request impossible specificity:
    What were the exact enrollment numbers for each section of PSYCH 100 at University of Illinois in Fall 2023?

    Does it admit it doesn't know, or does it provide "data"?

Try This: Detecting Bias

Test how AI handles the same prompt with different demographic cues:

Write a letter of recommendation for a software engineering position for a student named Jamal who excelled in my computer science course.

Now try the exact same prompt but change "Jamal" to "Emily"

Compare: Are there differences in tone, adjectives used, or focus areas? Does one emphasize technical skills more than the other?

Building Your Personal Guardrails

Based on what you've learned, let's create guidelines for YOUR AI use:

üìã My AI Guardrails Checklist

Check the items that make sense for your work context:

  • I will always verify factual claims, citations, or data before using them
  • I will not input confidential, personal, or sensitive information into AI tools
  • I will review AI outputs for potential bias, especially related to people
  • I will clearly indicate when work includes AI-generated content
  • I will use AI as a drafting/brainstorming tool, not a final authority
  • I will check my institution's AI policies before using it for official work

Additional guardrails for my context:

Key Takeaway: AI should augment human judgment, not replace it. Always be the critical thinker who verifies, questions, and makes final decisions.

üé® Activity 4: AI as a Creative Partner

15 minutes

Quick Concept: Temperature & Creativity

Many AI tools have a "temperature" or "creativity" setting (usually 0-1 or 0-2):

  • Low temperature (0-0.3): Predictable, consistent, factual‚Äîlike a careful researcher
  • Medium temperature (0.7-0.8): Balanced‚Äîgood for most writing tasks
  • High temperature (1.0+): Unpredictable, creative, varied‚Äîlike a brainstorming session

Note: Not all interfaces expose this setting, but understanding it helps you frame prompts!

Try This: Brainstorming with AI

Let's use AI for what it does well‚Äîgenerating lots of ideas quickly. We'll practice being the critical human who evaluates and selects.

Scenario: Course Activity Ideas

I'm teaching a 3-hour workshop on academic integrity for first-year undergraduates. Generate 10 different interactive activity ideas that would work for groups of 4-5 students. Make them diverse in format‚Äîinclude individual activities, small group discussions, role-plays, and hands-on tasks. Each idea should take 10-15 minutes.

Your job as the human: Review the 10 ideas and mark which ones are:

  • ‚úÖ Feasible for your actual context
  • ‚ö†Ô∏è Interesting but need modification
  • ‚ùå Not realistic or useful

Iterative Refinement

Pick one idea that was "interesting but needs work" and use this follow-up:

I like idea #[number], but [explain your concern or what needs to change]. Can you revise it to [specific improvement you want]?

This is where AI really shines‚Äîrapid iteration based on your expert judgment!

Try This: AI as Thought Partner

Use AI to challenge your thinking on a real problem you're facing:

I'm working on [describe your actual challenge or decision]. I'm leaning toward [your current approach]. Play devil's advocate: What are 5 potential problems or oversights with this approach? What alternative perspectives should I consider?

Remember: The AI doesn't know your context as well as you do. Its role is to generate perspectives‚Äîyours is to evaluate their relevance and accuracy.

Reflection: AI's Role in Your Workflow

üí≠ Think About It

  • Where would AI brainstorming genuinely save you time?
  • Where would you NEVER want to use AI, even for initial drafts?
  • How do you balance efficiency with maintaining your authentic voice and expertise?
Key Takeaway: Use AI for divergent thinking (generating many possibilities) but reserve convergent thinking (choosing the best option) for yourself. You're the expert‚ÄîAI is just a very fast brainstorming partner.
