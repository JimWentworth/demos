Simulacra

This section explores the concept of "simulacra"—representations or imitations that come to replace the very reality they were meant to represent. Jean Baudrillard outlined three "orders" or stages of simulacra, showing how the copy progressively detaches from the original. Interact with the cards below to see how this relationship evolves.

Simulacra (singular: simulacrum) are representations or imitations of people, things, or ideas that no longer have—or never had—a true original. The term is most often associated with Jean Baudrillard, who used it to describe how, in postmodern culture, symbols and signs replace reality itself.

Hyperreality

When the simulation no longer just represents reality but *becomes* our reality, we enter the state of hyperreality. It's a condition where the distinction between the real and the representation collapses. This section defines this crucial concept and its implications.

Hyperreality is the condition where the distinction between reality and its representation collapses—where simulations, media, and symbols feel more real or convincing than actual lived experience.

Baudrillard describes it as the state we enter when we interact with a world of models, images, and narratives that don’t point back to anything authentic, yet still shape how we perceive what’s “real.”

Think of examples like:

  • Theme parks designed to feel more authentically “American” than any real town.
  • Social media profiles curated until they seem more vivid than the person behind them.
  • AI-generated content that feels truer to human emotion than most human writing.

Hyperreality isn’t about deception—it’s about immersion in a convincing fiction that replaces the need for the original.

So, What is Reality Today?

After exploring simulacra, hyperreality, and modern tech, we're left with a fundamental question. This final section offers a perspective on what "reality" might mean in our heavily mediated world, presenting it as a negotiated, multi-layered construction.

Maybe the honest answer is that “reality” today is negotiated more than observed.

It’s no longer a stable ground we stand on—it’s a shared construction, constantly revised through algorithms, feeds, and interfaces. We experience what’s most legible to our systems and our circles, not necessarily what’s most real.

If you borrow Baudrillard’s lens, we now live inside a stacked reality:

Symbolic Systems

Memes, narratives, and reputations that decide what counts as truth or meaning.

Digital Layers

Data dashboards, news feeds, and recommendation systems that interpret and reframe events.

Material Events

The base layer of bodies, weather, and hardware that still happens.

“Reality” persists, but it’s filtered, ranked, and aestheticized before it reaches us. The challenge isn’t to peel back to some pure version; it’s to stay aware of which version we’re inhabiting, and who benefits from its construction.
